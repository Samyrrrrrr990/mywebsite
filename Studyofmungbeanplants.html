Introduction

Plants play a crucial role in the lives of humans and the environment through their involvement in the carbon cycle. They perform photosynthesis, using sunlight to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen. This process not only provides food for the plant itself but also releases oxygen into the atmosphere, which is essential for the respiration of animals and humans. Cellular respiration is the complementary process where plants and animals convert glucose and oxygen back into carbon dioxide, water, and energy, which is used for growth and other vital activities.

Plants require several factors for survival and optimal growth, including sunlight, water, carbon dioxide, and nutrients such as nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. Nitrogen is a critical component of chlorophyll, amino acids, and other cellular constituents, making it a vital nutrient for plant growth. However, the amount of nitrogen can significantly affect plant growth, as both deficiency and excess can be detrimental.

This study investigates the effect of different nitrogen levels in fertilizers on the growth quality and time of mung bean plants. The purpose of this lab is to understand how nitrogen levels influence plant growth, with the goal of contributing to sustainable agricultural practices.

Key Terminology/Background Information

Photosynthesis: The process by which plants use sunlight to synthesize foods from carbon dioxide and water. Photosynthesis in plants generally involves the green pigment chlorophyll and generates oxygen as a byproduct.

Cellular Respiration: The process in which cells break down glucose and other molecules from food in the presence of oxygen to produce energy.

Fertilizer: A chemical or natural substance added to soil or land to increase its fertility and promote plant growth.

Nitrogen: An essential nutrient for plants, a major component of chlorophyll, amino acids, and other cellular structures.

Chlorophyll: The green pigment in plants that absorbs light energy for photosynthesis.

Nutrient Uptake: The process by which plants absorb nutrients from the soil.

Growth Rate: The speed at which a plant increases in size over a period.

Median Growth: The middle value in a set of growth measurements, indicating the central tendency of plant growth data.

Purpose

The purpose of this lab is to investigate how varying levels of nitrogen in fertilizers affect the growth quality and time of mung bean plants, with the aim of identifying optimal conditions for their development.

Hypothesis

If mung bean plants are given a moderate amount of fertilizers with appropriate levels of zinc/nitrogen, then they will grow better than those given fertilizers with high or low nitrogen levels because an appropriate amount of nitrogen is essential for healthy plant growth.

Materials

  • Low-nitrogen fertilizer (4%)(3.5ml each day )
  • Medium-nitrogen fertilizer (7%)(3.5ml each day )
  • High-nitrogen fertilizer (12%)(3.5ml each day )
  • Water (16.5 ml each day)
  • Soil(500g each )
  • Mung bean seeds (3 per pot)
  • Humidity/pH level and sunlight gauge

Procedure :

Four mung bean plants were observed over ten days ( due to death of plants ) in a controlled environment at room temperature. Each plant received 16.5 ml of water and 3.5ml of fertilizer daily, except on weekends. Three plants were given different fertilizers: liquid all-purpose plant food, Quick Start, and Multi-garden fertilizer, applied according to package instructions. One plant served as a control with no fertilizer. Plant growth was monitored and recorded daily.

Data / Graph Analysis :

The graphs and data show how mung bean plants grew with different fertilizers. The plant with no fertilizer had the most stable growth. Fertilizers made plants grow faster at first, but then their growth became unstable. The plant with medium fertilizer grew quickly at the beginning, shown by the higher median growth. However, it also died faster, which is why its maximum height was less. The median value, which is the middle point of the growth data, shows that the medium fertilizer helped the plant grow more in the middle of the experiment.

Connecting Images to graphs :

The data tables and images provide more information about how fertilizers affect the mung bean plants. By looking at the color and number of leaves, it can be seen that the plant with medium fertilizer had more leaves than the one with no fertilizer. This means the medium fertilizer helped the plant produce more leaves. However, having more leaves also seemed to make the plant weaker and more likely to die early. The images show that the plant with medium fertilizer died sooner than the plant with no fertilizer. This indicates that while fertilizers can make plants grow faster and produce more leaves, they might also make the plants less healthy in the long run.

Discussion

Role of Plants in Ecosystem:

  • Oxygen Production: Plants produce oxygen through photosynthesis, essential for the survival of most living organisms.
  • Food Source: They provide food for herbivores, which in turn are food for carnivores and omnivores.
  • Nutrient Recycling: Through decomposition, plants recycle nutrients back into the soil, enriching it for future plant growth.
  • Habitat and Shelter: Plants offer habitat and shelter to various species, from insects to birds and mammals.
  • Soil Stabilization: Plant roots help prevent soil erosion by holding the soil together.
  • Water Cycle Contribution: Through transpiration, plants release water vapor into the atmosphere, playing a crucial role in the water cycle.

Best Growth:

  • The non-fertilized plant grew the best, likely because it received a balanced supply of natural nutrients without the interference of chemicals found in fertilizers.

Worst Growth:

  • The plant treated with high nitrogen fertilizer grew the worst, possibly due to nitrogen toxicity, which can harm plants by disrupting their nutrient uptake.

Unexpected Outcome:

  • The non-fertilized plant outperformed the fertilized plants, suggesting that the natural nutrients in the soil were adequate for optimal growth without the need for added fertilizers.

Additional Factor:

  • Testing different levels of zinc could provide insights into its role in mung bean growth. Optimal zinc levels might enhance growth, whereas too little or too much could be detrimental.
  • Exploring the effects of other micronutrients like iron, manganese, and copper could also be valuable in understanding plant growth variations.

Characteristics and Importance of Loam:

  • Balanced Texture: Loam is a balanced mix of sand, silt, and clay, which provides the ideal texture for plant growth.
  • Excellent Drainage: Loam allows water to drain well, preventing waterlogging, which can damage plant roots.
  • Nutrient Retention: It holds nutrients effectively, making them available to plants over time.
  • Good Root Penetration: The structure of loam enables easy root penetration, helping plants establish strong root systems.
  • Aeration: Loam’s texture provides good aeration, crucial for root health and microorganism activity.

Complementary Processes:

  • Photosynthesis: Occurs in chloroplasts, producing oxygen and glucose from sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water.
  • Cellular Respiration: Occurs in mitochondria, using oxygen and glucose to produce energy, carbon dioxide, and water.
  • These processes are interdependent; photosynthesis supplies the oxygen and glucose needed for cellular respiration, while respiration releases the carbon dioxide and water used in photosynthesis.

Sources of Error:

  • Equipment Accuracy: Using unreliable pH and moisture testing device might  have affected the accuracy of the data.
  • Fertilizer Forms: The different forms of fertilizer (liquid vs. solid) may have impacted the results. Making sure  consistent forms and application methods will control this variable better.
  • Environmental Factors: Variations in light intensity, temperature, and water availability could have influenced plant growth. Controlling these factors in future experiments can provide more accurate data.
  • Human Error: Measurement and observation errors could have influenced the results. Implementing rigorous training and standardized protocols can help minimize these errors in future studies.

Conclusion

The hypothesis that mung bean plants with moderate nitrogen levels would grow better was not supported. The non-fertilized plant showed the best growth, indicating that natural soil nutrients might be sufficient for optimal growth. This study highlights the need for balanced fertilization practices and suggests further research into other nutrients like zinc. These findings can inform sustainable agricultural practices, ensuring optimal crop production without over-reliance on chemical fertilizers.
